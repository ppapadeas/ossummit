Today we are going to take a view on what motivates people to contribute to open source, and we will be doing that by examining volunteerism and its relation with open source. Mozilla's experience for over 15 years in community building can help us articulate those motivations, and it is the source of most of today's points.

Volunteerism

Photo by angsbacka
Now let's talk about volunteerism. In their basic nature, open source contributions are a form of volunteerism. As such they inherit the inner workings of a volunteer contribution system together with their set motivations for participation. There is extensive literature for volunteerism, nethertheless we should cover the basics behind incentivization of volunteers.

Altruism

Photo by jopeattie
One of the basic incentives that drive volunteers can be altruism. The sense of selflessness and the urge to help others, most of the time inspired by the mission of the project, is definitely a strong motivation, and one that most volunteer projects are trying to build up most.

Socializing

Photo by ppapadeas
The sense of belonging in a group, making new friends and having fun can be also a strong motivation for volunteers. In general do not underestimate the power of socializing especially among volunteer groups.

Learning and Development

Photo by opensourceway
Learning and development can be another key theme on defining motivations for volunteer projects. Learning and polishing new skills, gaining work experience, receiving professional training and even developing leadership abilities are things that contributors might be looking into when joining a project.

Rewards and Recognition

Photo by Benny Chandra
A final point on volunteering motivations is rewards and recognition. Non-monetary rewards and public recognition through communication channels owned by the project can act as a token of appriciation from the project to the contributor. A special mention about swag in specific : Many would disregard them as incentivization mechanism, but I can assure you that they can play a key role early on engaging with the contributor. So up until now, we covered the basic set of motivations for volunteerism, but what happens with Open Source projects?

Open source contributions as we said can be considered as a subset of volunteering, but perhaps most importantly they can be an extension of it.

Photo by zamzara
So what is it that sets apart open source contributions from volunteering?

Global

Photo by Apollo 17
Most open source projects are cultivated out in the public with participation from all over the world. Their nature enables it. It is virtual, open to everyone and largely anonymous. A pregraduate student from Mombai can join a freelance programmer from Santiago de Chile to contribute to your projects. It is this celebration of diversity and global thinking that drives many people to participate in open source projects.

Access to data

Photo by Semion
Due to the nature of many open source projects, detailed and otherwise not available documentation, specifications and data, needs to be accessible to the prospective contributors. This access to data intrugues many of them and can act as a great motivation.

Participatory governance

Photo by Daniel Rose
When an open source project is deployed as participatory throughout its structure, the participatory governance poses a luring opportunity for people with leadership skills to participate and shine. Being part of the decision making procedure is fascinating for many contributors.

Exclusive

Photo by ruiwen
Open source projects have many times highly specifc and exclusive requirements in terms of technical skills from their contributors. Sometimes even us, as community builders have trouble identifying them and articulating them. It is this exclusivity and uniqueness that intrigues many contributors to join and be creative with like-minded people.
